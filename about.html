About

I move between the big question
and the fine detail
without losing either.

I came to product design through print — a decade of annual reports, luxury invitations, and event materials. Work that had to be right because you couldn't push an update after it went to press.

That shaped something in me: I can't think about craft without thinking about strategy, and I can't think about strategy without eventually needing to see it made. I work best when I'm holding both at once.

These days that looks like enterprise workflows, growth systems, and platform integrations — across iOS and web, always close to product and engineering, trying to make things feel clear, empathetic, and just a little bit delightful.

Chapter One

Art Director, Print & Brand

Ten years designing for print annual reports, luxury invitations, event materials, brand systems. Work with weight and texture. I developed a deep instinct for how visual decisions shape the way people feel about what they're reading, holding, or attending.

What print taught me: hierarchy, proportion, and craft aren't decorative choices they're how information communicates. That conviction carries into everything I do in digital.

Chapter Two

MFA, Experience Design — VCU Brandcenter

After a decade of making things look right, I started asking whether the things I was making were actually working for the people using them. The more I explored UX, prototyping, and systems thinking, the clearer the decision became. I applied to graduate school.

I enrolled in VCU Brandcenter's intensive graduate program 24 weeks pregnant. I became the first new mother in the program's history. In my second semester, COVID hit.

I finished anyway. What that period taught me about working under constraint, finding clarity in chaos, and moving forward without a map I use it every single day.

Chapter Three

Senior Product Designer — Skillshare

Skillshare is where I grew up as a designer. I joined as an intern and worked my way to Senior Product Designer leading the pod — the kind of trajectory that only happens when the team around you is genuinely good.

The collaboration was one of the best parts. I worked closely with people across product, engineering, design, legal, teacher support, customer support, and the brand studio. That breadth taught me how to communicate across very different contexts and still move work forward.

The product itself was genuinely complex: a learning marketplace with subscription mechanics, an M&A integration, and the kind of interconnected systems where a change in one place ripples everywhere. I learned to love that kind of problem.

Key work included the Certifications & Badges system that drove a +89% increase in project submissions, and the Creator Hub integration following the Superpeer acquisition.

Chapter Four

Senior Product Designer — Blaze AI

Joined Blaze as the second designer at a Series A AI startup. I wanted to understand how a smaller team operates in a fast-moving AI environment, and to experiment with how design itself could work differently.

I used AI across every stage of the design process, from early research to prototyping, gradually moving away from static files toward a more fluid, exploratory way of working. When I was curious about something, I dug in with AI, had direct conversations with the internal team who were the primary users of the enterprise product, and kept moving.

Worked across growth projects including pricing tiers, freemium flows, and credits. Established the iOS design system. Led the enterprise approval workflow, a feature that had to hold together everything the company was simultaneously experimenting with.

Design Principles

01

Clarity over cleverness.

A design that makes someone think "of course" is worth more than one that makes them think "impressive." But clarity doesn't come from instinct alone — it comes from context. User research, competitive analysis, behavioral data. The more I understand the person on the other side, the more inevitable the right answer becomes.

02

The messy middle is the work.

The polished screen is the last 10%. The real design happens in the ambiguity, the constraint, the conversation no one wanted to have. Tools change, AI moves fast, but the judgment you bring to that middle part is still yours.

03

Aesthetics are not decoration.

How something looks shapes how it feels to use. Visual craft isn't separate from UX rigor — it's part of the same decision. When both are right, people don't notice either. They just feel like they're in good hands.
